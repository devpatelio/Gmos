WHAT ARE GMOS GMOs are organisms whose genetic material has been altered to fit a specific set of features or genotypes. In other words, an organism whose genetic material has been altered using genetic engineering techniques. In this process, they are altered through genetic engineering in order to offer some advantage for the organism in its native habitat and environment. To create them, one must isolate the genes they insert into the host organism and combine it with other genetic elements! These genes can be transferred within the same species, across species (transgenic species), and kingdoms. They can also be introduced into a population to alter natural selection while endogenous/native genes can be enhanced, altered, or knocked out of the population!

Insecticides and GMO Correlation


One of the key advantages GM crops offer to farmers and consumers is the lack of insecticides
in order to defend against pests. All commercial GM crops have insect-resistant traits derived
from the bacteria Bacillus thuringiensis which produced toxins specialized for certain pests.
This ultimately reduced the cost of insecticides and its usage. Not only did it make farming
more sustainable but also kept the environmental impact of biomaginification at a minimum.
The significance of Bt crops can be seen through this graph which shows the inverse
relationship between insecticide usage and Bt corn.

Insectide usage is damaging to the envrionment and ever since the introduction of Bt corn,
more than 86% of all farms in North America have not adopted insecticides and pesticides.
This not only prevents traces of dangerous chemicals from reaching the consumer but also
keeps farmers and the food safer and cleaner. Many chemical pesticides include
organophosphates, carbamates, pyrethroids, and sulfonylureas which can result in poisoning,
especially farmers. The toxin that the Bt crop produces is deadly to the insect's digestive
system yet harmless to humans and other organisms, making biopesticides important for
more sustainable farming practices.


Growth in Popular GM Crop Traits


The growth of GM crops is seen across time. Many of the current crops that are genetically
modified are necessary staples across the world and are subjected to extreme temperature
and climate conditions. Soy and cotton for example are mostly genetically modified and for
good reason as the majority of these crops are produced in developing countries that do not
have access to industrial agriculture techniques. This enables them to produce more food for
their communities and improve their overall capital and economic situation.

Additionally, traits that can aid in this are herbicide-tolerance and insect-resistance. Herbicide
tolerance allows farmers to kill off any weeds through herbicides without poisoning or harming
their yields. It is far more efficient and reduces maintenance costs. Herbicide tolerance specifically
defends against glyphosate based herbicides which are the most common in an industrial and
agricultural based economy. Herbicides especially have reduced manual labour and made the crops
more resistant to external factors. The need for herbicide-tolerance has led to the industry increasing
by 7-8% annually since 1997.


GMOs Offer Potential to Developing Nations


GMOs are promised to lift up nations that have a primarily agricultural based economy. Not only does it
make their primary sector more efficient, but the impacts are scalable resulting in more supply for the
world, greater profits and living standards for the majority of the population, and a more productive
economy. This is no more apparent than in the case of India which saw its cotton exports increase
substantially by 100 million bales in just 3 years.

From 2001 and onward, India's total cotton exports grew from 7 billion to 21 billion USD in the first 5
years, and reached 35 billion USD by 2014. India additionally saw the majority of its agriculture sector
grow in this short time and it directly improved the living standards in rural India. Growth like this is the
benefit of GM crops especially for developing countries and allows them to have a presence at an intern-
ational level and raise the floor from an economic standpoint.

What are GMOs?

An introduction to GMOs

INTRODUCTION More than 75% of consumers know what GMOs yet almost all admit they do not understand what they really are apart from being believed to have a negative impact on their health. The term genetically engineered or modified may raise many red flags yet GMOs are an appropriate and necessary practice that has so much potential to improve the world’s current agriculture and food situation. A GMO is defined as an organism whose genetic material has been altered to fit a certain set of features or genotypes. An organism’s genetic material is altered through genetic engineering in order to offer some advantage for the organism in its native habitat and environment. While the ethics of this technology are heavily and continually debated they are undoubtedly useful in modern day agriculture.

The first GM food was a tomato in 1994 followed by GloFish in 2003 which was the first genetically modified animal approved.

SCIENCE To produce GMOs, the organism is altered in a way that does not occur naturally through either reproductive offspring or recombination. Genes can be transferred within the same species, across species (transgenic species), and across kingdoms making them useful for reproduction and adaptation to certain geographical areas but also makes them risky. Many GMOs can be introduced into a population to alter natural selection while endogenous/native genes can be enhanced, altered, or knocked out of the population. Previously, GMOs were largely non commercialized and expensive, but with genomic sequencing and editing techniques like CRISPR make it available and cheap. By taking foreign genetic material, CRISPR can alter base pair arrangements to embed this material. Many of our current plants and products are genetically modified in order to prevent drought conditions, improve taste, be prone to insects, and more.

GMOs can have a significant impact on the environment and ecosystems as a whole, potentially disrupting entire food webs if germination were to spread GM seed across an ecosystem.
